Miróbriga Ruins

October 14, 2018
Heritage

My first time in Miróbriga Ruins was to make a presentation related to the use of technologies among archaeological sites. Me and my colleague Alexandino Gonçalves were kindly invited by the archaeologist José Carlos Quaresma, in 2017. Back then, I felt in love with the cultural and nature richness of this archaeological site. So I came back, over and over again.

Classified as a Site of Public Interest in 1940, the archaeological site at Miróbriga has seen under the Portuguese Architectural Heritage Institute since 1982.

The city

Considered by some investigators as a sanctuary and by others as a provincial urban centre, Miróbriga was inhabited from, at least, the Iron Age to the fourth century AD. It is believed that this land rich in agricultural, maritime, and mining resources, had great commercial importance. Miróbriga probably acquired the rank of municipality during the Flavian dinasty (1st century AD).

From the 1st to the 3rd century A.D. the city shows an enourmous vitality and intense occupation, which tends to decline in the second half of the 4th century A.D.

Prior to Roman occupation and Iron Age settlement was found in the highest zone of Miróbriga by people of possible Celtic origin.

The full extension of this site is not yet known. However, it presents some remarkable features, such as the forum, the residential and commercial zones, the bath complex, the bridge, the stone paved streets, and most of all, its circus, where bigae and quadrigae races took place drawn by Lusitanian horses, famous throughtout the Roman world.

The houses have not yet been studied in depth. Some of them were decorated with frescoes and can be seen along the causeways that crisscross the urban mass, as the example shown in the following figure.

The Forum

At the highest level of the urban area, a forum was built over the previous occupation. This public square was the centre round which the buildings constituting the poilitical, administrative, economic, and religious centre of the town took their place. Occupying an important position in the forum is a centralized temple dedicated to Venus.

To the south, was the commercial area with various shops, tabernae. These were usually small rooms that opened directly onto the street. In some cases they had a parcial upper story, mezzanine, where the shop owner lived, steps led from the street to the main square.

The spa, one of the best preserved in Portugal

The Miróbriga spa has two buildings and they contain the usual compartments for this type of construction: entrance with room for clothes and games, the frigidariu or cold bath, the caldarium, and the tepidarium, the heated area.

Under the heated rooms was a hypocaust - an underflood structure of brick pillars and arches where hot air from a furnace circulated - most of the rooms have double walls for hot air circulation, as you can see in the figure below.

Quite close to the spa is a single arch bridge which probably linked the town to the hippodrome on the outskirts. The Miróbriga hippodrome (the venue for equestrian events) is the only example known to date in Portugal.

Ending Notes About Miróbriga

About Anabela Marto
Anabela Marto is an Invited Lecturer in the Computer Engineering Department at the Superior School of Technology and Management (ESTG) - Polytechnic of Leiria (IPLeiria) - and a Researcher at Computer Science and Communication Research Centre (CIIC). Her research topics are related to Mobile Augmented Reality applied in Cultural Heritage contexts. She is graduated in Computer Engineering (ESTG - IPLeiria), has a master degree in Multimedia - specialization in Technologies (FEUP - Faculty of Engineering of the University of Porto) and she is conducting her PhD work in Informatics (UTAD - University of Trás-os-Montes and Alto Douro).
